## 🎬 How to Present This

### In Essays

**Example phrasing:**

> "To bridge theory and practice, I built a decision-training platform that simulates supply chain crises. Unlike static case studies, my system generates infinite unique scenarios using stochastic modeling, allowing users to develop intuition for uncertainty. The platform tracks behavioral patterns and provides counterfactual analysis—showing not just what happened, but what optimal decision-making would have achieved."

### In Interviews

**30-Second Pitch:**

> "I noticed that business students study crisis management in cases, but rarely practice making decisions under real uncertainty. So I built a simulation platform that generates infinite unique crisis scenarios—each with hidden variables and probabilistic events. Users make a series of strategic decisions, and the system analyzes their behavior patterns, identifies cognitive biases, and shows them the gap between their performance and optimal strategy. It's like a flight simulator for strategic decision-making."

**Follow-up Points If Asked:**

- **Technical sophistication**: "I designed a stochastic event engine with hidden variables—just like reality, users don't see all the factors influencing outcomes"
- **Behavioral insight**: "The system detects patterns like loss aversion—for example, if someone consistently delays action to avoid short-term costs"
- **Practical application**: "Each simulation ends with a detailed feedback report showing decision timeline, bias analysis, and counterfactual—what would've happened with optimal strategy"

## 💬 FAQs You Might Get

**Q: "Why supply chain? Why not finance or marketing crises?"**

A: "Supply chains are perfect for demonstrating systems thinking—they have interconnected variables, time delays, and external shocks. The same architecture extends to any crisis domain. I started here because of my interest in operations, but the framework generalizes."

**Q: "How is this different from a case study?"**

A: "Three ways: First, infinite variation—no two runs are identical. Second, behavioral analysis—it doesn't just show outcomes, it shows patterns in how you decide. Third, counterfactual comparison—it shows what optimal would've looked like, so you learn not just from your path, but from roads not taken."

**Q: "Could this be used for training in companies?"**

A: "Absolutely. The modular design means you could customize scenarios for specific industries—retail, manufacturing, tech. Add industry-specific KPIs and events. The core engine stays the same, but context adapts. That's actually a potential business model."

**Q: "What was the hardest part technically?"**

A: "Balancing realism with playability. Real crises have hundreds of variables; I had to distill that to 5-6 core KPIs while still creating meaningful tradeoffs. I also had to calibrate the stochastic events—too many and it feels random, too few and it's deterministic."
